🌿 Plant Systems Overview

Plants have three major systems that interact to keep them alive and thriving.

💧

Transport System

Moves water, minerals, and sugars throughout the plant using xylem (water up) and phloem (sugars everywhere). The three tissue types — dermal, ground, and vascular — all play a role.

🌸

Reproductive System

Flowers, fruits, and seeds allow angiosperms to be the most successful plant group. Pollination leads to fertilization, producing seeds that can be dispersed widely.

☀️

Response System

Plants respond to light (phototropism), gravity (gravitropism), and touch (thigmotropism) using hormones like auxins, gibberellins, and cytokinins.

🧬

Plant Characteristics

All plants are eukaryotic autotrophs — they make their own food via photosynthesis. They have cell walls made of cellulose, contain chloroplasts with chlorophyll a & b, and are primary producers in food webs.

🪴

Plant Organs

Tissues group into organs: Stems (support, vascular highway), Leaves (photosynthesis, gas exchange), Roots (anchor, absorb water/minerals), and Flowers (reproduction). The shoot system (stems, leaves, flowers) and root system work together.

Transpiration & Wilting

Transpiration — plants lose water vapor through stomata. This creates a "pull" that draws water up through xylem.
Wilting — when water loss exceeds uptake, turgor pressure drops and leaves droop. Stomata close to prevent further water loss (abscisic acid triggers this).

🔬

Stomata & Homeostasis

Stomata are pores (mostly on the leaf underside) controlled by guard cells. They open to let in CO₂ and release O₂ for photosynthesis, but every opening also loses water — a trade-off. Plants regulate stomata opening/closing to maintain homeostasis.

🛡️

Dermal Tissue

Outer covering. Includes the epidermis and waxy cuticle that prevents water loss. Contains stomata with guard cells for gas exchange.

🌿

Ground Tissue

Majority of the plant. Three subtypes: Parenchyma (mesophyll, photosynthesis), Collenchyma (flexible support), Sclerenchyma (rigid support).

🚰

Vascular Tissue

Transport highway. Xylem carries water up (dead cells), Phloem carries sugars throughout (living cells). Together they form the plant's "veins."

Angiosperms are the world's most successful plants — thanks to flowers, fruits, and seeds!

🌸

Pollination

Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma. Occurs via wind, animals, or insects. Pollen grain grows a pollen tube down the style to the ovule.

⚡

Double Fertilization

Angiosperms have TWO fertilization events: one produces the zygote; the other produces the endosperm (triploid 3n) which nourishes the seed.

🍎

Fruit & Seed

A fruit is a matured angiosperm ovary containing seeds. After fertilization, nutrients from the vascular system flow to support the growing embryo.

🪴

Vegetative Reproduction

Asexual reproduction — new plants form by mitosis. No gametes, flowers, or fertilization needed. Methods: cuttings and grafting.

☀️ Response System

Plants respond to their environment using hormones and tropisms — no nervous system required!

🧪 Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)
🌿

Auxins

Promote cell elongation. Collect on shady side → plant curves toward light = phototropism. Produced in canopy → root growth.

📏

Gibberellins

Promote stem elongation, germination of seeds, root growth, and flowering.

🔀

Cytokinins

Mainly promote cell division. Work with auxins to regulate growth.

🍅

Ethylene

Stimulates fruit ripening. A gas — fruit produces it and it spreads to neighbors.

😴

Abscisic Acid

"Stress Hormone" — inhibits cell division, promotes seed/bud dormancy, closes stomata during drought.

🌀 Tropisms — Responses to External Stimuli
☀️

Phototropism

Growth toward light. Driven by auxin concentrating on the shaded side, causing cells there to elongate more.

⬇️

Gravitropism

Roots grow downward (positive) with gravity; shoots grow upward (negative) against gravity.

🤝

Thigmotropism

Response to touch or contact with a solid. Vines wrap around supports; ivy climbs walls.

🌙

Photoperiodism

Response to the length of day vs. night. Controls flowering in short-day and long-day plants.

⚡

Rapid Movements

Mimosa pudica folds leaves on touch; Venus flytrap snaps shut — triggered by electrical signals & water pressure changes.

🔗

Systems Connection: How Response Interacts with Transport & Reproduction

Hormones (auxins, ABA) are produced in one tissue and travel through phloem to affect others — linking the response system to the transport system. Photoperiodism triggers flowering — linking the response system to the reproductive system.

🌲 Four Major Types of Plants

Plants are classified by whether they have vascular tissue and how they reproduce.

🌿 Non-Vascular Seedless

No vascular system. Very short. Always in humid environments — water moves by diffusion & osmosis only.

🌿 Mosses, Liverworts, Hornworts (Bryophytes)
No xylem/phloem No seeds Humid only
🌾 Vascular Seedless

Have vascular tissue (xylem & phloem) but no seeds. Larger than mosses. Still need humid environments for reproduction.

🌿 Ferns, Club Moss (Pteridophytes)
Xylem + Phloem No seeds Spores
🌲 Gymnosperms

Vascular with seeds in cones (naked seeds). Among the largest trees on Earth. Prefer cold, dry environments.

🌲 Pines, Ginkgo, Cycads
Naked seeds Cones Cold/dry
🌺 Angiosperms

The most successful group! Seeds enclosed in fruit. Flowers attract pollinators. Can live in almost any environment.

🌸 All flowering plants — roses, oaks, grasses, crops
Flowers Seeds in fruit Most diverse
🏆

Why are Angiosperms so successful?

Three key innovations: (1) Flowers attract specific pollinators for efficient pollen transfer. (2) Fruit aids seed dispersal by animals. (3) Seeds contain endosperm to nourish embryos. Together these give angiosperms huge reproductive advantages.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Master these terms for the STAAR exam.

Xylem
Dead tubular cells that transport water and dissolved minerals upward from roots to leaves via capillary action and transpiration.
Phloem
Living tubular cells that transport sugars (sucrose) from leaves to all parts of the plant (source → sink).
Transpiration
Process by which plants lose water vapor through stomata in their leaves.
Stomata
Tiny pores in the cuticle (mostly on leaf undersides) that allow gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ out). Controlled by guard cells.
Photosynthesis
Process in chloroplasts that converts CO₂ + H₂O + light energy into glucose (sugar) and O₂.
Auxins
Plant hormones that promote cell elongation; responsible for phototropism by concentrating on the shaded side.
Gibberellins
Plant hormones that mainly promote stem elongation, germination, root growth, and flowering.
Cytokinins
Plant hormones that mainly promote cell division.
Ethylene
A gaseous plant hormone that stimulates fruit ripening.
Abscisic Acid
The "stress hormone" — inhibits growth, promotes seed dormancy, closes stomata during drought.
Phototropism
Growth toward light. Auxin concentrates on the shaded side, causing those cells to elongate more.
Gravitropism
Roots grow downward (positive gravitropism), shoots grow upward (negative gravitropism) in response to gravity.
Thigmotropism
A plant's response to touch or physical contact with a solid object (e.g., vines wrapping around a support).
Pollination
Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma, occurring via wind, animals, or insects.
Angiosperms
Flowering plants — the most successful group, with seeds enclosed in fruit. Reproductive success due to flowers, fruit, and seeds.
Alternation of Generations
The shift between haploid gametophyte stage (produces gametes by mitosis) and diploid sporophyte stage (produces spores by meiosis).
Meristems
Areas of actively dividing, unspecialized cells responsible for plant growth. Types: Apical, Intercalary, Lateral.
Pressure Flow Hypothesis
Theory explaining phloem transport: sugar loaded at source → water follows by osmosis → pressure drives flow to sink.
