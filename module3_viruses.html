The Immune Response to Disease
Understanding viruses and how the body fights back — from barriers to targeted immune attacks
⭐ STAAR KEY CONCEPT: Viruses are NOT living organisms — they are non-cellular particles that can only replicate inside a host cell. The body has two layers of defense against viruses and other pathogens: nonspecific defenses that respond to ANY threat, and specific immune defenses that target a particular pathogen.
What Is a Virus?
A Virus IS…
A non-cellular infectious particle — not a cell, not alive
Made of genetic material (DNA or RNA) wrapped in a protein coat called a capsid
Sometimes surrounded by a lipid envelope stolen from the host cell membrane
Much smaller than any cell — typically 20–300 nm (nanometers)
Capable of rapid mutation — why flu vaccines must be updated annually
An obligate intracellular parasite — can only reproduce inside a living host cell
A Virus is NOT…
Not alive — viruses do not carry out any of the characteristics of life on their own
Not a cell — no cell membrane, no cytoplasm, no organelles, no ribosomes of their own
Not a prokaryote or eukaryote — viruses are in their own category, outside the classification of living things
Not treated by antibiotics — antibiotics target bacterial cell structures; viruses have none
Not capable of metabolism — cannot generate energy, grow, or respond to the environment independently
Not capable of reproducing alone — must hijack a host cell's machinery to replicate

Three Lines of Defense
01
Physical Barriers — Skin, mucus, cilia. Block pathogens from ever entering the body.
02
Nonspecific Internal Defenses — Inflammatory response, phagocytes, fever. Respond to ANY invader.
03
Specific Immune Response — B cells, T cells, antibodies. Target a specific antigen.
Nonspecific Defenses
Respond to any pathogen — no targeting required
Mast cells release histamines → increased blood flow
Phagocytes engulf and destroy bacteria and damaged cells
Blood vessels dilate, fluid leaks into tissue → swelling (inflammation)
Fast response — minutes to hours
No immunological memory formed
Specific Defenses (Immune System)
Targets a specific antigen on a pathogen's surface
B lymphocytes → humoral immunity (antibody production)
T lymphocytes → cell-mediated immunity (direct cell attack)
Slower first response — days to weeks
Immunological memory formed (memory B and T cells)
Secondary response is much faster and stronger
✅ Key Vocabulary: Antigen = foreign molecule that triggers an immune response. Antibody = protein made by B cells that binds to a specific antigen. Lymphocyte = B cell or T cell — the immune system's specific response warriors.

Viral Reproductive Cycles
How viruses hijack host cells — the critical difference between lytic and lysogenic cycles
⭐ STAAR KEY CONCEPT: Know the difference between lytic and lysogenic cycles! Lytic = destroys the host cell immediately (acute infection). Lysogenic = viral DNA merges with host genome for an indefinite period before activating (chronic/latent infection).

🔴 Lytic Cycle — Acute Infection

The virus enters, replicates rapidly, and destroys the host cell to release hundreds of new virions. Causes acute infections (symptoms appear quickly).

LYTIC STEP 1 OF 5

Attachment

The virus recognizes and binds to specific receptor proteins on the host cell's surface. This specificity determines which cells the virus can infect — for example, HIV binds specifically to CD4 receptors on T helper cells.

🔴
Host-range: Viruses can only infect cells with matching receptors — this limits which organisms they can infect.
🔴
This step is the target of many antiviral drugs that block receptor binding.
LYTIC STEP 2 OF 5

Injection

The virus injects its genetic material (DNA or RNA) into the host cell. The viral capsid (protein shell) remains outside. The viral nucleic acid now floats in the host cell's cytoplasm.

🔴
Bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria) literally inject DNA like a syringe — the capsid acts as a needle.
🔴
Animal viruses often enter the entire viral particle by endocytosis, then release their genome inside.
LYTIC STEP 3 OF 5

Replication

The viral DNA takes over the host cell's machinery. The host's ribosomes translate viral proteins, and the host's polymerases replicate viral DNA. The cell is now a virus-making factory.

🔴
The host cell's own genes are often shut down — all resources redirected to viral production.
🔴
Hundreds to thousands of copies of viral DNA and capsid proteins are produced rapidly.
⭐ STAAR TIP: Viruses are NOT alive — they are NOT cells and have NO metabolism. They can ONLY replicate inside a living host cell.
LYTIC STEP 4 OF 5

Assembly

New viral DNA and capsid proteins self-assemble into hundreds of complete new virions inside the host cell. The host cell is now packed full and under tremendous stress.

🔴
A single infected cell can produce 100–1,000+ new virions before bursting.
🔴
Assembly is typically spontaneous — viral components naturally find each other and click together.
LYTIC STEP 5 OF 5

Lysis — Cell Destruction

The cell bursts (lyses), releasing hundreds of new virions into the surrounding tissue. Each of those virions can now infect new host cells. The host cell is killed.

🔴
Result: Acute infection with rapid, severe symptoms — the immune system detects the damage immediately.
🔴
Examples: Common cold, flu, many bacterial infections caused by bacteriophages.
🔴
This cycle repeats exponentially — each wave of lysis infects more cells.
⭐ STAAR TIP: LYTIC = the cell is KILLED (think: "lyse" = to break apart). Causes ACUTE infection — symptoms appear quickly.

🟣 Lysogenic Cycle — Chronic (Latent) Infection

The viral genome merges with the host cell's chromosome and may remain dormant for an indefinite period. The host cell survives and replicates, copying viral DNA along with its own.

LYSOGENIC STEP 1 OF 4

Integration

The virus injects its DNA and, instead of replicating immediately, integrates its genome directly into the host cell's chromosome.

🟣
A viral enzyme called integrase cuts the host DNA and inserts the viral genome at a specific site.
🟣
The integrated viral DNA is now a permanent part of the host's DNA — it will be copied every time the cell divides.
LYSOGENIC STEP 2 OF 4

Latency — Silent Replication

The host cell continues to live and divide normally. Each time the host cell replicates its DNA, the integrated viral DNA is copied automatically — passed to all daughter cells. The virus is completely dormant and produces NO symptoms.

🟣
This can last for years, decades, or an entire lifetime — the virus waits in hiding.
🟣
Examples of latent infections: Chickenpox virus (VZV) can reactivate decades later as shingles. Herpes simplex (HSV) hides in nerve cells. HIV integrates into T cells.
⭐ STAAR TIP: LYSOGENIC = the cell SURVIVES. The viral DNA merges with the host genome and causes a CHRONIC or LATENT infection — no immediate symptoms.
LYSOGENIC STEP 3 OF 4

Induction — The Trigger

When the host cell is stressed — by UV radiation, certain chemicals, weakened immune system — an induction signal causes the integrated viral DNA to separate from the host chromosome. The lysogenic cycle ends and the lytic cycle begins.

🟣
Triggers include: UV light, DNA-damaging chemicals, stress hormones, immune suppression.
🟣
This explains why stress or illness can cause a herpes cold sore or shingles outbreak — the latent virus reactivates.
LYSOGENIC STEP 4 OF 4

Activation → Lytic Cycle

The excised viral DNA re-enters the lytic cycle — replicating rapidly, assembling new virions, and ultimately lysing the host cell. This is why latent viruses can suddenly cause severe acute illness after years of dormancy.

🟣
Clinical example: VZV causes chickenpox (primary lytic infection), then goes lysogenic in nerve cells for decades. Reactivation causes shingles — the same virus, now lytic again.
🟣
HIV reactivation in T-cells leads to AIDS as immune cells are destroyed en masse.
⭐ STAAR TIP: LYSOGENIC → LYTIC is a one-way door once induction occurs. The cell will be destroyed.

🔴 Lytic Cycle

Host cell fate: Destroyed (lysed)
Infection type: Acute — rapid onset, severe symptoms
Viral DNA: Replicates separately in cytoplasm
Timeline: Minutes to hours; fast exponential spread
Examples: Common cold, flu, bacteriophage infections

🟣 Lysogenic Cycle

Host cell fate: Survives and continues dividing
Infection type: Chronic/Latent — dormant, no symptoms until triggered
Viral DNA: Integrates directly into host chromosome
Timeline: Can remain latent for years or decades
Examples: Herpes (HSV), chickenpox/shingles (VZV), HIV
Immune Disorders & Vaccines
When the immune system misfires — and how vaccines recruit its power
⭐ STAAR KEY CONCEPT: Immune system problems arise when it overreacts (allergies, asthma, autoimmune disease) or fails to respond (HIV/AIDS). Vaccines work by triggering immunological memory without causing the actual disease.
Overreaction

Allergies

The immune system treats a harmless substance (allergen) — pollen, pet dander, peanuts — as a dangerous pathogen. Mast cells release large amounts of histamines, causing sneezing, itching, swelling. Antihistamines block this response.

Overreaction

Asthma

An overreaction in the airways of the lungs. Triggered by allergens, exercise, or cold air, asthma causes bronchial inflammation and muscle contraction that narrows the airways, making breathing difficult. Treated with anti-inflammatory inhalers.

Attacks Self

Autoimmune Disease

The immune system fails to distinguish "self" from "nonself" and attacks the body's own healthy cells. Examples: Type 1 Diabetes (attacks pancreatic beta cells), Multiple Sclerosis (attacks nerve myelin), Rheumatoid Arthritis (attacks joint tissue).

Failure to Respond

HIV and AIDS

HIV attacks Helper T cells — the coordinators of both humoral and cell-mediated immunity. As T cell count drops, the immune system loses the ability to fight infections. When T cells fall below 200/µL, the patient has AIDS, vulnerable to opportunistic infections.

💉 How Vaccines Work

Vaccines trick the immune system into mounting a full immune response — including forming memory B and T cells — without causing the actual disease. They use weakened pathogens, killed pathogens, or just the antigens (surface proteins) of the pathogen.

01
Vaccine delivers antigens into the body (no live pathogen)
02
Immune system mounts a primary response — B & T cells activated
03
Memory B and T cells are formed and persist long-term
04
If real pathogen attacks later, rapid secondary response destroys it before illness
✅ Memory is the Key: The primary immune response (first exposure) is slow — 1–2 weeks. The secondary response (after vaccination or prior infection) is 10–100× faster and stronger because memory cells are already primed and waiting.
