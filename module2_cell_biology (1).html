Two Types of Cells
Prokaryotic Cells
No true nucleus — DNA floats in nucleoid region
No membrane-bound organelles
Always unicellular
Smaller — 0.1–10 µm
Single circular strand of DNA
Bacteria only (Archaebacteria + Eubacteria)
Oldest cell type on Earth — ~3.5 billion years ago
Eukaryotic Cells
True nucleus with double membrane (nuclear envelope)
Many membrane-bound organelles
Mostly multicellular (some unicellular protists)
Larger — 10–100 µm
Multiple linear chromosomes
Plants, Animals, Fungi, Protists
Evolved FROM prokaryotes (Endosymbiotic Theory)
✅ Both cell types share: DNA (genetic material), a plasma membrane, cytoplasm, and ribosomes — these are the four universal features of all living cells.

Endosymbiotic Theory
How eukaryotes evolved from prokaryotes — the BIG IDEA for your STAAR exam
⭐ STAAR KEY CONCEPT: If a test question compares the Endosymbiotic Theory to other theories of origin, remember the bottom line — eukaryotes (more complex) evolved FROM prokaryotes (less complex). Greater complexity arose from simpler organisms. Mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria that were engulfed and became permanent residents of larger cells.

About 3.5 billion years ago, only prokaryotes (bacteria) existed on Earth. Over hundreds of millions of years, some larger prokaryotes engulfed smaller bacteria rather than digesting them. This mutualistic relationship gave rise to organelles — specifically mitochondria and chloroplasts — and eventually the first eukaryotic cell.

STAGE 1 OF 4

The Ancient World — Only Prokaryotes

Roughly 3.5 billion years ago, all life on Earth was prokaryotic — single-celled bacteria with no nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles, just circular DNA floating freely in cytoplasm.

Two key bacteria existed that would eventually become the organelles we see in eukaryotes today:

🟠
Aerobic bacteria — capable of producing energy using oxygen (cellular respiration). These would become mitochondria.
🟢
Cyanobacteria (photosynthetic) — capable of capturing sunlight to make energy. These would become chloroplasts.
STAGE 2 OF 4

Infolding + First Engulfment

A larger ancestral prokaryote began to fold its plasma membrane inward — creating a protected region around its DNA. This was the origin of the nuclear envelope, giving rise to a true nucleus.

This same larger cell began engulfing smaller aerobic bacteria rather than digesting them. The smaller bacteria survived inside the host cell, and a mutually beneficial relationship developed:

✅
The host cell provided protection and nutrients.
✅
The aerobic bacterium provided energy (ATP) through cellular respiration — far more efficiently than the host alone.
STAGE 3 OF 4

Ancestral Eukaryote Forms

The host cell now had a true nucleus AND engulfed aerobic bacteria that became permanent mitochondria. In plant lineages, cyanobacteria were also engulfed, eventually becoming chloroplasts.

Over millions of generations, the engulfed bacteria lost the ability to live independently — they became organelles. This was endosymbiosis: one organism living inside another for mutual benefit.

🔬
Key evidence: Mitochondria and chloroplasts both have their own circular DNA and double membranes — remnants of their bacterial ancestry.
🔬
They reproduce by binary fission, just like bacteria do.
STAGE 4 OF 4

Modern Eukaryote — Complexity Achieved

The result of endosymbiosis is the modern eukaryotic cell — with a true nucleus, mitochondria, and (in plants/algae) chloroplasts. Eukaryotes are far more complex than their prokaryotic ancestors.

The Big Picture for STAAR:

⭐
Greater complexity originated from less complexity. Simple prokaryotes gave rise to complex eukaryotes.
🔬
Evidence: mitochondria/chloroplasts have own DNA, double membranes, divide by binary fission, are similar in size to bacteria.
📅
Prokaryotes first appeared ~3.5 bya; eukaryotes ~1.8 bya — prokaryotes came first.

⭐ The Bottom Line — STAAR Exam Ready

The Endosymbiotic Theory explains that eukaryotes evolved from prokaryotes. This means more complex life arose from simpler life. On any STAAR question comparing this theory to others, anchor your reasoning here: the evidence (double membranes, own DNA, binary fission in mitochondria and chloroplasts) all supports that these organelles were once free-living bacteria that became permanently incorporated into a host cell.
